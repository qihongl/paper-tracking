Editorial Note

A strong day for hippocampal navigation and memory mechanisms. Two back-to-back Nature Neuroscience papers from July 1 independently demonstrate goal-directed theta sweeps during memory-guided navigation — Tang et al. (Fernandez-Ruiz lab) and Yu, Ji & Burgess et al. — providing converging evidence from rodent electrophysiology and computational modeling that hippocampal theta sequences flexibly shift from local sampling to goal-directed trajectory planning. This is directly relevant to the KV-Networks pillar's interest in key-to-position mappings in abstract spaces. Meanwhile, Frank & Strange et al. show human hippocampal ripples act as precision-weighting signals in a predictive coding framework, tuning cortical responses to expected uncertainty — a finding that bridges Pillars 2 and 3 by linking hippocampal oscillations to information-theoretic principles. On the LLM side, four arxiv preprints grapple with attention dilution, KV cache compression, and information asymmetry in causal attention, all of which bear on the lingering memory puzzle. The standout computational insight comes from Gollapudi et al., who formalize an "attention dilution effect" that degrades in-context retrieval as irrelevant documents flood the softmax — a mechanism-level discovery with direct implications for Pillar 1.

LLM-Memory
Can Language Models Actually Retrieve In-Context? Drowning in Documents at Million Token Scale
Gollapudi, Gupta, Singhal & Min (Min) · arXiv:2607.01538, Jul 1, 2026 LLM-Memory KV-Networks

Approach: Systematic study of in-context retrieval at million-token scale using BlockSearch, a 0.6B LM retriever with architectural and training modifications enabling length-generalization up to 10× beyond training regime. They trace retrieval collapse under extreme extrapolation to an attention dilution effect — irrelevant documents dominate the softmax denominator, reducing normalized mass on the gold document even when its pre-softmax score stays high. They introduce length-aware softmax adjustments and document-level sparse attention to counteract this.

Finding: At million-token scale, the modified model matches dense retrieval on MS MARCO and NQ while being 7× smaller than concurrent models, and achieves 3× higher scores on retrieval tasks requiring different similarity notions (LIMIT). The attention dilution effect represents a mechanism-level discovery: as context windows grow, the fundamental retrieval problem is not feature quality but normalizer dominance by distractors. This is directly relevant to the lingering memory pillar — it formalizes how attention-based memory retrieval degrades under interference, analogous to proactive interference in human episodic memory.

PARTREP: Learning What to Repeat for Decoder-only LLMs
Widjaja, Kim, Kim & Lee · arXiv:2607.01792, Jul 2, 2026 LLM-Memory

Approach: Addresses the information asymmetry problem in causal attention — later tokens are richer in contextual grounding than earlier ones. Proposes PartRep, which selectively appends only the most informative tokens (selected via NLL as a signal of contextual recoverability) rather than full prompt repetition. A lightweight gate trained on early-layer hidden states predicts high-NLL tokens, enabling selection during mid-prefill via early exit.

Finding: Across 8 benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K, RULER) and 3 model families (Qwen2.5, Llama3.2, Gemma4), PartRep retains most gains of full repetition while using only 59.4% of KV cache and 79.0% of prefill FLOPs. The core insight — that causally disadvantaged (early-position) tokens benefit disproportionately from late-position repetition — connects to the lingering memory paradigm's finding that information position in a sequence modulates accessibility.

⚠ Preprint — not yet peer reviewed. KV cache savings reported on synthetic benchmarks; real-world long-context robustness not tested.
Kara: Efficient Reasoning LLM Serving via Sliding-Window KV Cache Compression
Han & Wu · arXiv:2607.01237, Jul 2, 2026 LLM-Memory

Approach: Proposes Kara, a sliding-window KV cache compression method that operates only on recently generated context during decoding. Uses bidirectional attention within the window to score and select informative KV pairs, and a Token2Chunk module to expand selected pairs into flexible-sized chunks preserving semantic units. Built on vLLM with PagedAttention integration.

Finding: Improves throughput over existing threshold-triggered compression methods by avoiding rigid boundary chunking and by compressing only the recent decoding window. The bidirectional scoring mechanism is notable because it mimics the non-causal access to context that biological memory enjoys — this tension between causal (online) and bidirectional (offline) memory access is central to the lingering memory paradigm's V3–V6 experiments.

⚠ Preprint — not yet peer reviewed. Throughput improvements measured under specific hardware configurations.
CheckRLM: Effective Knowledge-Thought Coherence Checking in Retrieval-Augmented Reasoning
Xu, Wang, Zhao, Yan, Wang, Zha, Yu, Liu, Wang, Han & Sun · arXiv:2607.02262, Jul 3, 2026 LLM-Memory

Approach: Framework that detects factual errors in reasoning chains by extracting factual claims and cross-checking them against retrieved external knowledge. When inconsistencies are detected, a refinement mechanism performs minimal-cost corrections to maintain coherence between the reasoning chain and correct knowledge. Tested on knowledge-intensive long-horizon reasoning tasks.

Finding: Substantially outperforms baselines by mitigating error accumulation in long reasoning chains. The key mechanistic insight is that factual errors in early reasoning steps propagate and compound — a form of memory interference in chain-of-thought generation. This error accumulation dynamic parallels the pattern observed in the lingering memory experiments where prior ON trials conditionally degrade subsequent performance.

⚠ Preprint — not yet peer reviewed. Evaluation limited to English-language benchmarks.
Schema-Episodic
Goal-directed hippocampal theta sweeps during memory-guided navigation
Tang, Mei & Fernandez-Ruiz · Nature Neuroscience, Jul 1, 2026 Schema-Episodic KV-Networks

Approach: Recorded CA1 and PFC spiking activity in rats performing goal-directed navigation in an open arena. Distinguished two forms of theta sweeps: experience-independent left–right alternating sweeps during random foraging, and learning-dependent goal-directed sweeps that emerge after training. Identified a circuit mechanism: egocentric goal-direction signals combined with reduced feedback inhibition shift sweep direction from alternating to goal-directed.

Finding: Goal-directed theta sweeps predict upcoming trajectories, coordinate with prefrontal cortical activity, and are preferentially replayed during sharp-wave ripples — suggesting offline consolidation of goal-relevant sequences. The sweeps exploit distinct context-specific latent maps in hippocampus. This provides a neural mechanism for how the brain flexibly switches between local spatial sampling and goal-directed planning, and is directly relevant to the KV-Networks pillar's interest in key-to-position mappings — theta sweeps can be viewed as sequential key-based retrieval of spatial goal representations.

Hippocampal theta sweeps indicate goal direction during navigation
Yu, Ji & Burgess · Nature Neuroscience, Jul 1, 2026 (Open Access) Schema-Episodic KV-Networks

Approach: Independently converging finding from the Burgess lab. Analyzed hippocampal theta sweeps during memory-guided navigation, testing whether sweep direction encodes goal location independent of movement direction or head direction. Used computational modeling to link sweep properties to navigation performance.

Finding: Hippocampal theta sweeps encode goal direction independently of movement or head direction, and sweep properties correlate with navigation performance. Published in the same issue as Tang et al., these two papers together provide strong converging evidence — from different labs, using complementary methods — that theta sequences serve goal-directed spatial planning, not just local sampling. The independence from movement direction is critical: it shows the hippocampus maintains an abstract goal representation decoupled from motor output, consistent with cognitive map theories.

Human hippocampal ripples tune cortical responses based on predicted uncertainty
Frank, Moratti, Friston & Strange · Nature Neuroscience, Jun 25, 2026 Schema-Episodic Encoding-Retrieval

Approach: Direct intracranial recordings from human hippocampus and visual cortex during an visual stream task with varying entropy (predictability). Measured ripple frequency, duration, and timing relative to stimulus presentation; analyzed how ripples modulate cortical gamma activity and fusiform E/I balance. Included an oddball control experiment to dissociate predictability from surprise.

Finding: Hippocampal ripple frequency and duration increase before unpredictable (high-entropy) stimuli, not before surprising (high prediction-error) stimuli — a clean dissociation. Prestimulus ripples suppress occipital gamma increase associated with uncertainty and shift fusiform cortex toward a more balanced E/I state (steeper spectral slope). Poststimulus, ripple-primed trials show faster and stronger fusiform gamma responses to surprising stimuli. Crucially, ripple duration tracks expected information gain (entropy), not observed surprise — establishing ripples as a precision-weighting signal in a predictive coding framework. This directly connects to the Schema-Episodic pillar: schema-guided predictions shape hippocampal encoding via precision-weighting.

Time cells lead neural reinstatement of episodic memory
Dulaney, Seger, Carranza, MaheshKumar, Jacobs & Lega · bioRxiv, Jun 28, 2026 Schema-Episodic Encoding-Retrieval

Approach: Microelectrode recordings from human medial temporal cortex during two classical episodic paradigms — free recall and serial reconstruction. Identified time-sensitive neurons and tested whether they participate in context recovery during retrieval. Analyzed neuronal assembly organization on gamma and theta timescales, phase coding, and cross-task generalization.

Finding: Time cell firing precedes broader assembly activation and initiates sequential retrieval — consistent with temporal context models (TCM) where temporal context is recovered first and then drives item recall. Phase coding by complementary populations is recruited depending on task demands (free recall vs. serial reconstruction). Context-sensitive neurons emerge even in naturalistic viewing without predictable temporal scaffolds. These findings provide the first direct cellular evidence that time cells in human MTL play a causal role in episodic reinstatement, bridging the TCM computational framework with single-neuron data. Directly relevant to Pillar 2's schema-guided episodic memory RNN.

Old mice fail to integrate a memory update into an existing hippocampal engram
Brunswick, Defina, Wood, Baldwin, McKenna, Sicher, Marwaha, Brockway, Murakami, Pifer, Smies, Crowley & Kwapis · bioRxiv, Jun 22, 2026 Schema-Episodic

Approach: Investigated how memory engrams in dorsal hippocampus encode memory updates in young vs. old mice. Used activity-dependent tagging to label the original memory engram, then tracked re-engagement of those same neurons during an update session. Applied chemogenetic activation to test whether boosting engram re-engagement rescues updating deficits.

Finding: Old mice re-engage a smaller proportion of the original memory engram during updates, and chemogenetic enhancement of this re-engagement alleviates age-related updating deficits. Multiple behavioral and pharmacological approaches promoting training-engram re-engagement also improved updating. This identifies a specific neural mechanism for age-related memory updating impairment: failure to reactivate the original engram, not failure to form new associations. Relevant to Pillar 2's schema-guided memory framework — memory updating requires integration with existing schemas, and this integration machinery degrades with age.

KV-Networks
If Grid Cells are the Answer, What is the Question? A Review of Normative Grid Cell Theory
Dorrell & Whittington · eLife (Reviewed Preprint), Jul 3, 2026 KV-Networks Schema-Episodic

Approach: Comprehensive review of 20 years of normative grid cell theory. Synthesizes evidence from continuous attractor networks, efficient coding, path-integration constraints, and RNN models into a unified three-part account: grid cells are (1) biologically plausible, (2) high-fidelity nonlinearly decodable codes for position, and (3) subserve path-integration.

Finding: The central argument is that path-integration is the missing ingredient that distinguishes grid cells from purely efficient codes: the translational symmetry within grid modules (which is detrimental for efficient coding) is exactly what enables simple, module-wise path-integration. Only nonlinear decoding objectives combined with path-integration constraints produce realistic multi-modular grid codes. This is directly relevant to the KV-Networks pillar — it formalizes when and why key-to-position mappings adopt toroidal (modular) structure. The review also outlines lessons for normative modeling of other neural systems, with implications for how we should think about structured representations in artificial networks.

Analysis of dendritic input currents during place field dynamics
Fogel & Ujfalussy · eLife, Jul 3, 2026 KV-Networks Schema-Episodic

Approach: Develops an "extended currentscape" method to measure and visualize contributions of individual membrane currents to somatic responses in spatially extended biophysical model neurons. Applied to a CA1 pyramidal neuron model with place field inputs during traversal of a 2 m linear track, including theta modulation, phase precession, and 12 functional synaptic clusters.

Finding: Despite abundant localized dendritic events, membrane potential dynamics throughout the dendritic arbor are dominated by back-propagating action potentials (single factor explains ~70% variance). Complex spike bursts can occur at highly variable proximal input levels; strong distal (tuft) inputs facilitate but do not control burst generation. Tuft inhibition causes ~80% reduction in burst rates vs. ~65% reduction in spike rates. This provides a mechanistic account of how place field output depends on the interaction between proximal and distal inputs — relevant to understanding how spatial key representations drive hippocampal output.

Encoding-Retrieval
Early Visual Cortex Supports One-Shot Episodic Memory via Spatially Tuned Reactivation
Woodry, Winawer & Favila · eLife (Reviewed Preprint), Jul 3, 2026 Encoding-Retrieval

Approach: fMRI study (n=19 after exclusion) testing whether spatially tuned memory responses emerge in V1–V3 after a single encoding event. Participants viewed objects at one of four parafoveal locations, then completed object recognition (old/new) and location recall with foveal presentation. Used pRF encoding models and von Mises fits to measure spatial tuning fidelity, amplitude, and width during encoding, recognition, and recall.

Finding: Both recognition and recall evoked spatially tuned responses in V1–V3 peaking at the encoded location, even though recognition did not require spatial information. Effect sizes: encoding amplitude ~0.86% BOLD (~25× larger than memory responses); tuning width was 81° (encoding) vs. 122° (recognition) vs. 161° (recall). Fidelity was higher for remembered vs. forgotten items (recall: 0.35 vs. −0.03). Crucially, spatial tuning at encoding predicted subsequent memory (+0.11% BOLD difference). These results demonstrate cortical reinstatement after a single exposure — the defining property of episodic memory — with quantitative precision about the tuning width and amplitude profiles. Relevant to Pillar 1's questions about single-trial encoding in LLMs.

The precision of hippocampal representations predicts incremental value-learning across the adult lifespan
van Geen, Lempert, Cohen, MacNear, Reckers, Zaneski, Wolk & Kable · J. Neuroscience, Jun 26, 2026 Encoding-Retrieval Schema-Episodic

Approach: Large-sample study (n=251, ages spanning adulthood; n=76 with fMRI) using a trial-and-error reinforcement learning task where participants learned the value of four perceptually similar houses. Used representational similarity analysis (RSA) to quantify the precision of hippocampal stimulus representations during choice, and tested connectivity between posterior hippocampus and medial PFC as a function of age.

Finding: The precision of posterior hippocampal representations during choice predicted RL accuracy across age groups — a hippocampal contribution to incremental value-learning traditionally associated with striatal systems. In older adults, performance decline was not driven by degraded ("blurred") hippocampal representations (contrary to neural dedifferentiation predictions), but by reduced hippocampus–mPFC connectivity. This directly challenges the strict declarative/procedural memory dichotomy and shows that even simple RL depends on hippocampal representational precision. Relevant to Pillar 2's schema-episodic integration: precision of item representations in hippocampus gates value assignment.

Rhythmic replay of short-term memory neural patterns revealed by time-resolved error prediction
Syrov, Schmidt, Rademacher & Kobeleva · bioRxiv, Jun 22, 2026 Encoding-Retrieval

Approach: EEG study where participants encoded arrays of colored, oriented objects under two memory loads and reported features of a retrospectively cued item on a continuous scale. Used time-resolved multivariate pattern analysis to predict subsequent recall error from encoding- and maintenance-period activity, testing for rhythmic fluctuations in encoding fidelity.

Finding: STM encoding fidelity fluctuates rhythmically at theta frequency (~4–8 Hz). Fronto-parietal theta- and beta-band activity predicted subsequent memory error, and this prediction was not sustained but fluctuated rhythmically. Cross-temporal generalization showed the same neural pattern recurred across encoding and maintenance, and prediction fluctuations were temporally offset across spatial positions and object features. This characterizes STM encoding as a rhythmic, recurrent process — not a continuous one — with direct implications for how temporal scaffolds (theta cycles) organize memory encoding. Relevant to the lingering memory paradigm's temporal dynamics.

Cross-cutting
Learning shapes neural geometry in the primate prefrontal cortex
Wójcik, Stroud & Stokes · Nature Neuroscience, Jun 25, 2026 (Open Access) Cross-cutting

Approach: Recorded neural activity from macaque PFC while learning a new XOR rule from scratch, then generalizing to new stimuli. Tracked the geometry of neural representations across weeks: measured shattering dimensionality, selectivity profiles, and decoding accuracy for task-relevant vs. task-irrelevant features. Used feedforward network models with metabolic cost terms to explain the transition.

Finding: PFC representations progress through distinct stages: early learning produces high-dimensional, randomly mixed selectivity (all features decodable — a reservoir-like exploration regime), while late learning compresses to low-dimensional, rule-selective geometry (only task-relevant features survive). Upon generalization, representations further abstract into stimulus-invariant codes aligned along shared axes. The transition is driven by metabolic efficiency — minimizing total firing while maintaining performance. This reconciles conflicting accounts of PFC function by showing both high-dimensional and low-dimensional regimes are used at different stages. The finding has cross-cutting implications: (1) for Pillar 1, it suggests LLM representations may similarly transition from high-dimensional to compressed during task learning; (2) for Pillar 2, it provides a prefrontal complement to hippocampal schema formation; (3) for Pillar 3, the shared-axis generalization mechanism parallels key-based abstraction in KV networks.

Preserved geometry during representational drift enables stable perception and memory
Zaid & Schaffer · bioRxiv, Jun 25, 2026 Peripheral

Approach: Examines how neural representations can drift substantially over time while perceptual and mnemonic performance remains stable — the representational drift problem. Tests whether preserved representational geometry (rather than preserved individual neuron tuning) underlies behavioral stability.

Finding: Despite substantial representational drift at the single-neuron level, the geometry of population representations is preserved, enabling stable readout. This is conceptually relevant to the KV-Networks pillar's interest in how structured representations maintain function despite noise — the geometry, not the individual keys, matters for output.
