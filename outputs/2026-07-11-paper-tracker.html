LLM-Memory
Remember When It Matters: Proactive Memory Agent for Long-Horizon Agents
Yifan Wu, Lizhu Zhang, Yuhang Zhou, Mingyi Wang, Bo Peng, Serena Li, Xiangjun Fan, Zhuokai Zhao · arXiv, 2026-07-09 LLM-Memory

Approach: Introduces "behavioral state decay" — the failure mode where decision-relevant information (task requirements, prior attempts, open subgoals) gets buried or pushed out of the context window in long-horizon LLM agents. A separate memory agent runs alongside an unmodified action agent, maintaining a structured memory bank and deciding whether to inject a memory-grounded reminder or remain silent. The module is plug-and-play with existing agent harnesses. Trains Qwen3.5-27B using SFT and GRPO on a custom SETA dataset for open-weight memory policies.

Finding: Selective intervention outperforms passive bank exposure, always-on injection, advisor-only guidance, and general retrieval. Across Terminal-Bench 2.0 and τ2-Bench, pass@1 improves by +8.3 pp and +6.8 pp respectively for both weaker and stronger action agents. Ablations confirm that active, selective memory injection — not the mere presence of memory — drives the gain. This directly connects to the researcher's Pillar 1 question: when and how should contextual memory intervene to shape ongoing behavior, rather than simply being available for retrieval?

Multi-Head Recurrent Memory Agents
Jiatong Li, Samuel Yeh, Sharon Li (UW-Madison) · arXiv, 2026-07-01 LLM-Memory

Approach: Recurrent memory agents compress input into a fixed-size window iteratively, but suffer from systematic degradation. The authors decompose performance into memory capture (encoding fidelity) and memory retention (preservation across updates), quantitatively showing retention is the dominant bottleneck. Proposes Multi-Head Recurrent Memory (MHM), a training-free framework partitioning memory into independent heads with a select-then-update strategy: at each step, exactly one head updates while others are structurally shielded from overwriting.

Finding: On RULER-HQA at 896K tokens, MHM-LRU improves memory retention rate from <30% to 73.96%. Gains generalize across model families (tested on multiple architectures), scales, and task types. The key insight — that architectural shielding, not better model behavior, is the path to reliable long-context memory — is directly relevant to the KV-memory mechanistic questions in Pillars 1 and 3 regarding interference and overwriting in fixed-capacity memory systems.

Denser ≠ Better: Limits of On-Policy Self-Distillation for Continual Post-Training
Meng Wang, Haohan Zhao, Wenzhuo Liu, Lu Yang, Geng Liu, Haiyang Guo, Guo-Sen Xie, Gaofeng Meng, Hongbin Liu, Fei Zhu (HKISI-CAS, CASIA, UCAS, NJUST) · arXiv, 2026-07-02 LLM-Memory

Approach: Compares Self-Distillation Policy Optimization (SDPO) against GRPO for continual post-training of LLMs across multiple skill domains (math, coding, science, tool use). Measures forgetting across sequential training stages, parameter-space drift, and response-space drift. Analyzes how denser token-level supervision signals interact with the teacher-student feedback loop.

Finding: SDPO accelerates in-domain specialization when teacher signals are stable, but causes stronger forgetting and can collapse entirely in continual post-training. Denser self-distillation induces larger drift in both parameter and response space, amplifying high-frequency formatting artifacts through a self-reinforcing loop. GRPO adapts more conservatively and better preserves prior capabilities. This is a sobering result for the researcher's work on LLM memory: it shows that denser supervision signals can paradoxically increase forgetting, directly relevant to the interference dynamics studied in Pillar 1.

⚠ arxiv preprint, not yet peer-reviewed. Claims about "collapse" would benefit from broader model-scale validation.
Schema-Episodic
Beyond DSA: Conjugacy-based Comparison of Dynamical Systems
Prakhar Godara, Pang Shiang Tay, Marcelo G. Mattar (UC San Diego) · arXiv (q-bio.NC), 2026-07-05 Schema-Episodic

Approach: Identifies a fundamental limitation of Dynamical Similarity Analysis (DSA; Ostrow et al. 2023): orthogonal alignment of Koopman operators is neither necessary nor sufficient for topological conjugacy between dynamical systems. Proposes Conjugacy-based Similarity Analysis (CSA), which restricts alignments to those induced by candidate state-space bijections. Proves that CSA's fitted alignment is the finite-data projection of the composition operator, and demonstrates with controlled examples why this distinction matters for observable dictionaries.

Finding: CSA correctly distinguishes conjugate from non-conjugate systems where DSA fails (both false positives and false negatives). This is methodologically critical for the researcher's Pillar 2 work comparing RNN dynamics to hippocampal population dynamics: DSA-based similarity claims may be misleading, and CSA provides a principled, bijection-grounded alternative. From Mattar's lab, whose work on successor representations and planning has directly influenced the schema-guided episodic memory framework.

Arousal state modulates human hippocampal ripples
Elizabeth M. Siefert, Yvonne Y. Chen, Kathryn A. Davis, H. Isaac Chen, Anna C. Schapiro, Brett L. Foster (UPenn) · bioRxiv, 2026-06-26 Schema-Episodic

Approach: Direct intracranial recordings from human hippocampus during sleep and wake, with simultaneous arousal measurements via sleep staging, pupillometry, and heart rate. Tests the hypothesis that low arousal — not behavioral state per se — is the common mechanism of ripple genesis across species, explaining why human ripples appear during both offline sleep and active wake tasks.

Finding: Ripple occurrence consistently tracked low arousal across all measures: rates maximal in NREM sleep, elevated during small-pupil wake states and slow heart rate periods. Modulation was stronger in anterior than posterior hippocampus and was hippocampus-specific (ripple-like activity outside hippocampus showed the opposite modulation). This provides a unifying framework: "offline" consolidation occurs continuously, intermixed with cognition during transient arousal dips — directly relevant to Pillar 2's schema-guided episodic memory model and its consolidation dynamics.

⚠ bioRxiv preprint. Sample size and electrode coverage not fully detailed in abstract.
If Grid Cells are the Answer, What is the Question? A Review of Normative Grid Cell Theory
William Dorrell, James C. R. Whittington (Gatsby Unit, UCL / Oxford) · eLife Reviewed Preprint, 2026-07-03 Schema-Episodic

Approach: Comprehensive review synthesizing two decades of normative grid cell theory. Argues grid cells can be explained as (1) biologically plausible, (2) high-fidelity, nonlinearly decodable codes for position that (3) subserve path integration. Discusses why multimodal/combinatorial grid solutions require nonlinear encoding to be optimal, and draws lessons for normative theories of neural computation more broadly. Published as an eLife Reviewed Preprint with public peer reviews.

Finding: The normative framework that grid cells elegantly combine the easy path-integration of place cells with the high coding capacity of multifield cells — a "goldilocks" solution. The review clarifies a key debate: whether grid cells perform path integration or whether the grid pattern is the output of a path integration process. Relevant to Pillar 2 as a case study in how normative (top-down) and mechanistic (bottom-up) modeling can converge, and as a template for theorizing about hippocampal representational geometry in schema-guided memory.

KV-Networks
A Hippocampus for Linear Attention: An Exact Memory for What the Recurrent State Forgets
Wanyun Cui (Shanghai Univ. of Finance and Economics) · arXiv (cs.AI), 2026-07-02 KV-Networks

Approach: Explicitly inspired by Complementary Learning Systems theory, HOLA (Hippocampal Linear Attention) pairs a compressive delta-rule state (neocortical analog) with a bounded exact KV cache (hippocampal analog). The cache writes without a learned eviction module — it retains tokens with large β·||e|| (the prediction residual actually committed to the state). Decoupled RMSNorm-gamma cache read turns stored KV pairs into sharp retrieval rather than soft averaging. Trained at 340M params on 15B SlimPajama tokens.

Finding: HOLA lowers Wikitext perplexity from 27.32 to 22.92 (−16.1%), below a full-attention Transformer++ baseline (26.88). On RULER needle-in-a-haystack recall out to 32K tokens (16× training length), HOLA is substantially more robust than GDN or recency-cache baselines. This is the closest architectural instantiation of the CLS framework the researcher works with — a semiparametric test-time memory explicitly modeled on hippocampal-neocortical interaction. Highly actionable for Pillar 3.

SHiPPO: Recurrent Memory with Transported Polynomial Projections
Tomoya Mizuguchi, Bum Jun Kim · arXiv (cs.LG), 2026-07-03 KV-Networks

Approach: HiPPO gives recurrent states memory semantics as online polynomial projection coefficients, but in fixed channel coordinates. SHiPPO (Sylvester HiPPO) lifts HiPPO coefficient memories into a moving channel frame via right-transport — transporting both the approximation family and channel metric together. Derives restricted group-local realizations compatible with selective SSM controllers, exponential-adjusted updates, exact block-affine scan, and recurrent decoding.

Finding: Controlled diagnostics reveal that larger current-token write rank improves ordinary prediction error but cannot recover order-sensitive changes to already-written memory; transported-memory variants recover this signal. This has direct mechanistic implications for Pillar 3: it formalizes the distinction between write-strength (how much you update) and write-selectivity (whether you preserve order-sensitive information already stored), which is central to the KV-memory framework's account of serial position effects.

Encoding-Retrieval
Environment geometry alters sequential route learning and its integration into cognitive maps
Jaida Long, Estibaliz Herrera, Y. Li, Felipe Oliveira, Rida Ahmed, Sana Hussain, Camille Rivera, Kristin Liquori, Thackery Brown (Georgia Tech) · Nature Communications, 2026-07-07 Encoding-Retrieval

Approach: Virtual navigation task directly testing how environment geometry (trapezoid vs. square) and serial position in a route interact to influence spatial memory. Tests competing predictions: do geometric irregularities impair memory (grid cell distortion hypothesis) or aid it (orientation landmarks)? Controls for serial order effects predicted by path integration models.

Finding: Geometry and serial-order effects combine additively: location memory is worst toward route-end in the most geometrically irregular (trapezoid) environment. Self-reported navigational ability correlates with increased sensitivity to geometric irregularities. The additive combination of grid-cell-predicted and path-integration-predicted effects suggests these two spatial coding systems operate as independent, composable constraints on memory — directly relevant to the KV-networks framework's account of how position information interacts with event memory.

Cross-cutting
Neural timescales from a computational perspective
Roxana Zeraati, Anna Levina, Jakob H. Macke, Richard Gao (MPI Biological Cybernetics / U Tübingen) · Nature Neuroscience, 2026-07-02 Cross-cutting

Approach: Nature Neuroscience review synthesizing three computational directions: (1) how data analysis methods capture timescales across modalities (spikes, LFPs, calcium imaging, fMRI BOLD — spanning ms to ~10⁴ ms), (2) how biophysical mechanistic models explain the emergence of diverse timescales (cellular, network, input-driven, neuromodulatory mechanisms), and (3) how task-optimized models (RNNs, SSMs, LSTMs) uncover functional relevance. Introduces the critical distinction between τ_fit (data-estimated autocorrelation time constant) and τ_param (model internal parameter).

Finding: Proposes τ_fit as a comparable "fingerprint" bridging biological and artificial systems — directly enabling the kind of brain-DNN comparison the researcher's lab performs. Argues that "representational neural timescales" mirroring environmental fluctuation timescales may be necessary (not merely sufficient) for perception, memory, planning, and action. This framework provides theoretical scaffolding for all three pillars: timescale diversity in LLM attention (Pillar 1), hippocampal-cortical consolidation dynamics (Pillar 2), and temporal context in KV memory (Pillar 3).

Peripheral
Sequential Correlations Change In-Context Learning: Effective Context Length and Architectural Mismatch
Mary Letey, Yue M. Lu, Cengiz Pehlevan, Jacob Zavatone-Veth (Harvard) · arXiv (stat.ML), 2026-07-04 Peripheral

Approach: Extends ICL theory from i.i.d. prompts to sequentially correlated data (a basic feature of real sequences like language). Builds a solvable model based on linear attention and validates predictions on realistic transformer architectures. Identifies two distinct effects of within-context correlations on ICL performance.

Finding: Within-context correlations induce an effective context length reduction (correlated prompts behave like shorter i.i.d. prompts), but query-context correlation reduces test error — and this benefit is larger for softmax attention than linear attention. This formalizes a temporal-context effect in ICL: the sequential structure of examples matters not just for what is learned but for how efficiently it is learned, with architectural implications for which attention mechanism is best matched to natural sequential data. Adjacent to the researcher's interest in temporal context effects in LLM memory.
