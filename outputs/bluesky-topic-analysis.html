1. Neural Replay & Consolidation

3 papers
Studying Replay in Humans: A TICS Forum
Nico Schuck, Stephen Fleming, Marit Petzka · TICS, 2026-07-07
Argues that human replay tools (MEG/fMRI) can answer questions rodent recordings cannot — including replay content specificity, relationship to conscious experience, and individual differences.
FASTIMAGES: A Combined MEG + fMRI Replay Detection Benchmark
Simon Kern et al. (CIMH Mannheim) · Preprint, 2026-06-15
Builds a ground-truth benchmark with known neural sequences so replay-detection algorithms can finally be validated against objective truth rather than inferred patterns. Critical methods contribution for the replay field.
Planning in the Brain: It's Not What You Think It Is
Marcelo Mattar & Nathaniel Daw · Annual Review of Neuroscience, 2026-04-21
Proposes that the brain's "planning" machinery — including hippocampal replay — is primarily used for learning from simulated experience, with prospective decision-time thinking as a special case. Reframes replay as a credit-assignment mechanism.

2. Naturalistic Paradigms & Event Cognition

2 papers
Age Differences in Neural Timescales During Movie Viewing
Nichole Bouffard et al. · bioRxiv, 2026-05-07
Uses naturalistic fMRI (movie watching) to show age-related changes in hippocampal neural timescales, related to memory specificity. Suggests the hippocampus may not be uniquely specialized in this respect — a provocative null finding.
Event Structure Dynamics with Repeated Film Viewing
Narjes Al-Zahli, Chris Baldassano, Mariam Aly · J. Neuroscience, 2026-05-07
Tracks changes in event structure representations at multiple timescales during repeated film viewing. Brain regions' representations become coarser or finer with familiarity; slow-timescale structure predicts memory.

4. LLMs, Modularity & NeuroAI

4 papers
Modularity Emerges in Both Human Brains and LLMs
Pengrui Han, Andrea Devarda, Jacob Andreas · Preprint (shared by Ev Fedorenko), 2026-06-30
Finds that the same modular organization observed in human cortex (distinct networks for language, formal reasoning, social reasoning) emerges spontaneously in LLMs, suggesting modularity may be inevitable in intelligent systems.
Memorization vs. Generalization in AI
Andrew Lampinen (Google DeepMind) · Blog post, 2026-02-18
Surveys evolving perspectives on memorization vs. generalization from classic ML through modern LLMs. Argues the relationship is more nuanced than a simple trade-off — relevant to the "lingering memory" question in Pillar 1.
Escape Dimensions: A Theory for Why Larger Networks Train Better
Flavio Martinelli et al. · Preprint, 2026-06-10
Proposes an alternative to the lottery ticket hypothesis grounded in optimization theory: larger networks train better because they provide more "escape dimensions" from poor local minima.
NeuralSet: A Scalable Neuro-AI Package
Jean-Rémi King (Meta AI) · Tool release, 2026-04-21
Python package for aligning neural data (fMRI, EEG, MEG, iEEG, spikes) with AI model embeddings (text, audio, video, images). Directly useful for your own NeuroAI analyses.

5. Schema, Prior Knowledge & Episodic Memory

3 papers
Prior Knowledge Biases Episodic Memory by Filling Gaps in Imprecise Memories
Michele Ramey et al. · Cognition, 2026-06-10
Shows that prior knowledge fills gaps when episodic memories are imprecise — a schema-driven reconstruction effect. Simulates aging effects in younger adults by manipulating memory precision. Directly relevant to Pillar 2.
Neural Circuits Encode Prior Knowledge of Temporal Statistics
Dirk Gütlin et al. · Nature Neuroscience, 2026-04-08
Demonstrates that neural circuits — particularly cerebellum — encode learned temporal statistics as prior knowledge that shapes subsequent processing. Bridges Bayesian predictive coding with circuit-level mechanisms.
Non-Constituent Priming: Evidence That Human Memory Is Vast and Implicit
Matt Bury, Morten Christiansen et al. · Nature Human Behaviour, 2026-02-09
Demonstrates priming of non-hierarchical language structures, arguing against the traditional linguistic assumption that memory is a scarce resource. Human memory operates via compression, clustering, and implicit statistical learning.

6. Working Memory & Individual Differences

2 papers
Cerebellar Perturbation Impairs Working Memory Representations Cortex-Wide
Brissenden, Taraz Lee et al. · bioRxiv, 2026-05-28
Combines fMRI, TMS, and computational modeling for causal evidence that cerebellum contributes to brain-wide working memory representations and recall. Expands the WM network beyond the canonical prefrontal-parietal circuit.
LTM Encoding Predicted by Inter-Electrode Correlations, Independent of WM
Chong Zhao, Monica Rosenberg et al. · Imaging Neuroscience, 2026-05-05
Using iEEG, shows that individual differences in LTM encoding are uniquely predicted by inter-electrode correlations even after controlling for WM abilities — suggesting WM and LTM encoding are dissociable abilities with distinct neural signatures.
